# Clean Energy — Sector Framework

_Created: 2026-05-18 | Review quarterly or after major sector events_

---

## Why This Sector Exists in the Portfolio

The energy transition is not a policy story — it's a physics and economics story. AI data centers are projected to consume 1,000+ TWh annually by 2030, more than doubling the baseline demand for electricity in the US. That power has to come from somewhere that is reliable, scalable, and increasingly carbon-constrained. The result is a structural premium on dispatchable, clean generation: nuclear first, then gas with carbon capture, then storage + intermittent renewables.

The investment thesis across names in this sector is not "climate policy will force demand." It's narrower: **AI and electrification are creating a power scarcity that utilities, grid hardware makers, and next-gen energy providers are uniquely positioned to monetize regardless of political cycle.** VST's nuclear plants and GEV's grid hardware don't need a new subsidy regime — they need load growth, which is already contracted and incoming.

The secondary thesis layer is the nuclear renaissance. After 30 years of stagnation, nuclear is regaining social license due to AI power demand, energy security, and the hard math of intermittency. Microreactors (OKLO) and gigawatt-scale nuclear (VST, 7011.T) are both in the portfolio because the thesis holds at both ends of the power curve.

---

## How Value Is Created in This Sector

**1. Scarcity of dispatchable clean power.** Nuclear, natural gas with carbon capture, and pumped hydro are the only non-weather-dependent clean energy sources that can dispatch on demand. AI data centers require 24/7 firm power with <4 nines uptime — intermittent solar and wind cannot meet this spec alone. Operators with dispatchable clean assets (VST, OKLO, 7011.T) command structural pricing premiums that are increasing, not decreasing, as load grows.

**2. Grid infrastructure as a mandatory upgrade cycle.** The US grid was built for unidirectional power flow and moderate voltage variability. Distributed solar, offshore wind, and large-scale AI loads are creating voltage instability, congestion, and transformer shortfalls that require trillion-dollar grid investment. GEV's grid solutions business (transformers, HVDC, grid software) is a picks-and-shovels play on that upgrade cycle — it doesn't matter which energy source wins.

**3. Storage as the intermittency bridge.** Long-duration storage (batteries, hydrogen, gravity) is the mechanism that makes renewable-heavy grids viable. FLNC (battery storage) and BE (hydrogen-enabled fuel cells) are positioned for this bridging role. The margin structure is lower than generation assets, but the TAM is enormous and the unit economics are improving rapidly.

**4. International regulatory arbitrage.** Germany (ENR.DE) and Japan (7011.T) face energy policy regimes that incentivize specific technologies earlier than the US market does. These positions provide exposure to regulatory tailwinds that operate on different cycles, providing partial portfolio diversification.

## Industry Structure — Value Chain Map

| Layer | Role | Key Players | Moat Type | Margin Profile |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Dispatchable Generation** | Nuclear, gas, firm clean power | VST, 7011.T | Asset scarcity + license-to-operate | Very high, recurring |
| **Next-Gen Nuclear** | Microreactors, SMRs | OKLO | IP + regulatory first-mover | Early stage, binary |
| **Grid Hardware** | Transformers, HVDC, inverters, grid software | GEV | Manufacturing scale + installed base | Medium-high |
| **Long-Duration Storage** | Grid-scale batteries, BESS integration | FLNC | Software + integration expertise | Low-medium, improving |
| **Fuel Cells** | Stationary power from hydrogen or natural gas | BE | Process IP + installed base | Medium, recurring service |
| **Wind Development** | Onshore/offshore wind project development | ENR.DE | Site permits + local market expertise | Medium, project-based |

**Structural insight:** The highest near-term returns are in assets that already generate cash (VST, GEV) while the highest long-term optionality sits in next-gen nuclear (OKLO). Storage and fuel cells (FLNC, BE) have the largest TAM but are structural losers in a world where nuclear and grid buildout satisfy demand before long-duration storage reaches cost parity.

## Company Archetypes and How to Evaluate Them

### Archetype 1: Nuclear-Anchored Utility (VST, 7011.T)

_Dispatchable clean power assets operating into a structurally tighter market_

**What matters most:** Power purchase agreement (PPA) pricing vs. spot market (PPAs lock in value; spot exposure provides upside in tight markets), capacity factor at nuclear plants (nuclear should run >90% — anything below signals operational issues), regulated vs. deregulated revenue mix, dividend trajectory and cash return policy.

**VST-specific:** Vistra's nuclear fleet (Comanche Peak, Braidwood, Byron, etc.) is the core thesis. The Energy Harbor acquisition added Ohio/Pennsylvania nuclear to the portfolio. Watch: AI hyperscaler direct PPA wins (named customers = re-rating catalyst), capacity market prices (forward indicator for earnings), and Texas ERCOT tight margins (VST's largest deregulated market).

**7011.T-specific:** Mitsubishi Heavy's clean energy exposure spans SMRs (participation in next-gen designs globally), hydrogen (industrial scale), and LNG turbines. The Japan context means nuclear restarts are a primary catalyst — every idled reactor that restarts is material earnings uplift.

**Valuation:** EV/EBITDA for operating utilities (8–14x fair range for nuclear-anchored operators). When AI PPA wins are announced with long-duration contracts, apply a premium multiple to that contracted revenue stream.

**Warning signs:** Nuclear plant operational issues (NRC notices, capacity factor drop), regulatory risk in key markets (Illinois, Texas), hyperscaler PPA negotiations stalling, spot electricity prices collapsing.

---

### Archetype 2: Next-Gen Nuclear (OKLO)

_Binary option on microreactor commercialization — size for total loss in bear case_

**What matters most:** NRC licensing timeline (OKLO's Aurora design is the first microreactor to file a combined license application — approval is the primary de-risking event), anchor customer LOI conversion to binding contracts, manufacturing cost roadmap vs. grid parity, capital raise trajectory vs. first-unit deployment.

**What to track:** Number of signed letters of intent (LOI) and conversion to firm purchase agreements, partnership announcements (DoD, data center operators are the primary early customers), cash runway vs. NRC approval timeline, any NRC RFI responses or RAI resolution.

**Valuation:** Probability-weighted scenario analysis. OKLO is pre-revenue with no product shipped. Assign probabilities to: regulatory approval on-time (45%), 12-month delay (35%), rejection/restart (20%). In the bull case (commercial units deployed 2028–2030), OKLO's TAM in data center + DoD power is multi-billion. Position size accordingly.

**Warning signs:** NRC denial or material request for additional information (RAI) that resets the timeline by 2+ years, anchor customer cancelling LOI, competing microreactor design reaching NRC approval first, management departures from the engineering team.

---

### Archetype 3: Grid Infrastructure Hardware (GEV)

_The mandatory upgrade pick-and-shovel — every grid transition scenario requires GEV products_

**What matters most:** Grid Solutions segment order backlog (transformers and HVDC are 18–36 month lead time — backlog is the primary forward indicator), Wind revenue trajectory (offshore wind = higher margin; onshore = more competitive), Power segment (gas turbines for flexible generation — demand recovering with AI load growth), electrification service revenue (recurring, sticky).

**GEV-specific dynamics:** GE Vernova was spun from GE in April 2024. The spin cleaned up the balance sheet and created a pure-play energy transition vehicle. The key debate: is Wind a drag on capital allocation or an improving business? Monitor Wind segment margin trajectory and any strategic decision to slow investment there. The Grid Solutions business is the crown jewel — transformer lead times extending to 3+ years is a persistent supply constraint that keeps pricing power.

**Valuation:** EV/EBITDA or EV/Revenue. Grid Solutions comps are premium industrial businesses (20–28x EBITDA). The Wind drag may suppress the blended multiple below intrinsic value — look for separation if management provides segment-level targets.

**Warning signs:** Grid Solutions backlog declining (demand pause), Wind losses widening beyond guidance, transformer supply constraint resolving faster than expected (destroys pricing power), foreign competitors (Siemens, ABB) winning flagship US grid contracts.

---

### Archetype 4: Battery Storage / BESS Integrator (FLNC)

_Software-differentiated storage integrator competing on project delivery and AI-optimized dispatch_

**What matters most:** Project backlog and book-to-bill ratio, gross margin per MWh deployed (improving as hardware costs fall and software revenue grows), software attach rate (Mosaic OS software layered on top of hardware projects is the high-margin layer), repeat customer rate (energy developers rebooking = customer satisfaction signal), cash flow from completed projects vs. working capital required for pipeline.

**Valuation:** EV/Revenue or EV/Backlog during the ramp phase. FLNC is not yet consistently profitable — the key metric is gross margin trend, which should inflect positive as software mix increases and commodity battery pricing falls.

**Warning signs:** Gross margin declining despite increased scale (cost control failure), battery procurement issues (supply chain disruption), competitor undercutting on hardware-only contracts, software differentiation failing to sustain premium pricing.

---

### Archetype 5: Fuel Cell Operator (BE)

_Stationary power for data centers and industrial — mission-critical uptime, recurring service revenue_

**What matters most:** Accepted (commissioned) power generation units (AGS) — the installed base metric, annual service revenue vs. product revenue mix (service is higher margin, recurring), efficiency improvement roadmap (efficiency improvements = lower operating costs = more competitive vs. grid alternatives), data center customer concentration (early adopters set the template for the market).

**Valuation:** EV/Revenue during growth phase, transitioning to EV/EBITDA as service revenue compounds. Bloom is best evaluated as a subscription infrastructure business once the installed base reaches scale.

**Warning signs:** New product acceptance rates slowing, service contract renewal rates declining, efficiency improvements lagging roadmap, hydrogen supply chain disruptions if hydrogen-pathway products gain share, data center customers opting for grid power as reliability improves.

---

### Archetype 6: International Wind Developer (ENR.DE)

_Project development economics: sites + permits + grid connection = value creation_

**What matters most:** MW in construction pipeline vs. target (execution track record), project IRR on recent completions (benchmark: 8–12% unlevered IRR for mature onshore wind), feed-in tariff or auction contract pricing in Germany and adjacent markets, grid connection lead times (bottleneck in Germany is grid queue, not turbine supply).

**Valuation:** NAV-based — discount cash flows from operating projects + risked value of development pipeline. At 1.0–1.3x NAV, reasonably priced for the German regulatory environment.

**Warning signs:** Germany grid connection queue extending beyond 3 years (stalls construction), turbine cost inflation outpacing auction clearing prices, regulatory changes to German EEG subsidy structure, permitting denials in core markets.

## Where We Are in the Industry Cycle

**Current phase: Demand inflection + supply constraint (2025–2028)**

AI power demand is pulling forward grid investment timelines by 5–7 years. What was planned as a 2030–2035 grid modernization is being pulled to 2026–2030 by contracted hyperscaler load. The key characteristics of this phase:

- Transformer lead times at 3–4 years (GEV backlog building)
- Nuclear PPA pricing at record premiums ($100–130/MWh vs. 2021 levels of $40–60/MWh)
- Microreactor LOI pipeline growing but first commercial unit 3–4 years from deployment
- Battery storage costs continuing to fall (LFP < $80/kWh at pack level in 2025)
- European wind capacity market still clearing at premium prices driven by energy security demand

**Signals that this phase is evolving:**
- AI capex deceleration (reduces power demand pull-forward thesis)
- Transformer manufacturing capacity expanding to normalize lead times (2027+ risk)
- NRC approval or denial for OKLO (binary event resets the nuclear SMR narrative)
- German grid interconnection queue resolving (unlocks ENR.DE pipeline)

**The 2028–2030 transition:** As grid hardware supply catches up, margin pressure on GEV's Grid Solutions will emerge. Winners will be those with the lowest-cost power assets (VST nuclear, OKLO if licensed), recurring service revenue (BE, FLNC software), and contracted international pipelines (ENR.DE, 7011.T).

## Sector Bull / Base / Bear Cases

**Bull case (40%):** AI power demand pulls forward 10+ years of grid investment into the next 4 years. VST wins 3+ named hyperscaler nuclear PPAs at $120+/MWh. OKLO receives NRC approval and announces its first commercial deployment by 2028. GEV's Grid Solutions backlog grows to $30B+. German grid queue accelerates with federal mandate.

**Base case (45%):** Orderly AI infrastructure buildout continues with moderate power demand growth. VST nuclear PPAs priced at $100–115/MWh. GEV delivers on backlog at 15–18% EBITDA margins in Grid Solutions. OKLO NRC approval in 2027 with first commercial unit 2029. FLNC reaches profitability by FY2027 as software mix increases.

**Bear case (15%):** AI capex pullback reduces near-term power demand urgency. Nuclear PPA pricing resets lower as grid supply catches up. OKLO NRC application rejected, requiring redesign and restart (3–5 year delay). GEV Wind losses widen materially, absorbing Grid Solutions cash flow. German energy policy reversal cuts ENR.DE project economics.
