## Investment Thesis

> **Thesis established:** May 19, 2026
> **Last validated:** May 19, 2026
> **Drift status:** On track — initial thesis established; monitor G2_Austin ramp and FEOC compliance status

T1 Energy Inc. (NYSE: TE) is building the largest integrated domestic solar supply chain in the United States. Its G1_Dallas facility operates at 5 GW of module assembly capacity today; its G2_Austin facility (5.3 GW in two phases) began construction in December 2025, with Phase 1 (2.1 GW) targeted for production by end of 2026. The strategic aim is vertical integration from polysilicon to finished module — sourcing Corning wafers from Michigan, Hemlock polysilicon, and Nextpower domestic steel frames — to achieve >60% domestic content and qualify for IRA Section 45X production tax credits at full value. Q1 2026 revenue was $177.6M (+232% YoY), with record adjusted EBITDA of $9.1M as the company shifted its contract mix from merchant sales toward fixed-margin and cost-plus offtake structures.

The bull case is regulatory moat through FEOC compliance. The Inflation Reduction Act and the subsequent One Big Beautiful Bill Act created a bifurcated solar market: modules with foreign entity of concern (FEOC) exposure face exclusion from federal procurement and certain utility contracts, while FEOC-compliant producers capture a premium on domestic content. T1 is structuring its supply chain and ownership specifically to qualify — a defensible position if the compliance certification holds. At full G1+G2 integration (10 GW), management targets $650–700M in annualized adjusted EBITDA run-rate. Upon G2 Phase 1 completion alone, the target is $375–450M EBITDA run-rate — implying a 2–3x current revenue base at margins that justify the current $1.6B market cap.

The moat is supply chain infrastructure and FEOC certification, not technology. T1's modules use crystalline silicon technology licensed from Trina Solar — the differentiation is provenance (U.S.-assembled, domestic content) rather than efficiency or cost per watt. This is a compliance-and-policy moat: durable as long as FEOC rules remain enforced and domestic content mandates apply, but vulnerable to policy reversal or Chinese competitor workarounds.

Key risks are structural and near-term. Trina Solar (Schweiz) AG holds 17.97% of shares — a Chinese-affiliated entity — creating persistent FEOC scrutiny and requiring ongoing corporate restructuring to maintain 45X credit eligibility. T1 completed a complex compliance restructuring in early 2026, but the relationship is inherently fragile to regulatory reinterpretation. G2_Austin execution risk is real: fab construction delays, cost overruns, or ramp delays into 2027 would push back the EBITDA inflection. CEO Daniel Barcelo has held the role since November 2024 with limited track record at this scale. Two independent directors resigned from the board in March 2026 — governance signal worth monitoring.

**Key moat:** Largest U.S. domestic solar module manufacturer with FEOC-compliant supply chain (Corning wafers, Hemlock polysilicon); Section 45X tax credit eligibility; integrated G1+G2 production capacity of 10 GW — the infrastructure moat that takes years to replicate.

**Key risks:** Trina Solar ownership stake creates ongoing FEOC/45X compliance fragility; G2_Austin ramp execution risk; Section 45X credit policy exposure (legislative); CEO tenure <2 years with limited track record; low insider ownership (0.32%); board turnover (2 directors March 2026).
