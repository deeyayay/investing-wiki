## Investment Thesis

> **Thesis established:** May 31, 2026
> **Last validated:** May 31, 2026
> **Drift status:** On track — initial thesis established; monitor for Yb-176 revenue ramp and QLE HALEU licensing progress

ASP Isotopes Inc. owns exclusive global rights to two proprietary isotope enrichment technologies: the Aerodynamic Separation Process (ASP), a physical separation method effective for heavy isotopes, and the Quantum Enrichment (QE) Process, a laser-based approach optimized for HALEU (High Assay Low Enriched Uranium, 5–20% U-235 enrichment). The company targets three distinct commercial pillars: (1) medical isotopes — Ytterbium-176 (Yb-176), the precursor for Lutetium-177 targeted cancer therapy, and Molybdenum-100 for imaging; (2) quantum/semiconductor isotopes — Silicon-28 (Si-28), which dramatically reduces decoherence in spin-qubit quantum computing devices; and (3) nuclear fuel — HALEU, required by every advanced SMR design currently under development. In January 2026, ASPI closed the acquisition of Renergen Limited, adding South African natural gas liquids and commercial-scale helium production, which feeds the quantum computing and MRI cooling markets.

The structural bull case is the HALEU supply gap: Russia (TENEX) is presently the only commercial-scale HALEU supplier in the world. All advanced SMR reactor designs — TerraPower's Natrium, X-energy's Xe-100, Kairos Power's FHR, and Oklo's Aurora — require HALEU fuel and cannot run on conventional light-water reactor enriched uranium (<5% U-235). The Nuclear Energy Institute projects global HALEU demand exceeding 3,000 metric tonnes by 2035. ASPI has received interest from potential customers representing over $30B of HALEU demand at current market prices and has signed MOUs with at least two US-based SMR companies including TerraPower. The cross-ticker signal with OKLO is direct: ASPI could become a fuel supplier for OKLO's Aurora microreactors, which require HALEU. US government energy security policy provides a strong regulatory tailwind — DOE has active programs funding domestic HALEU enrichment development to break dependence on Russian supply.

The moat is a combination of proprietary technology and supply chain position: (1) exclusive global ASP and QE technology rights — no other company has access to these specific enrichment processes; (2) the NECSA (South African Nuclear Energy Corporation) partnership, which provides sovereign nuclear expertise, established enrichment infrastructure, and a licensed operating environment outside the US regulatory queue; (3) first-mover advantage in non-centrifuge HALEU — the competing Western HALEU approach (Centrus/LEU) uses gas centrifuge technology and has received DOE funding but is also not yet at commercial scale; (4) the Renergen helium business, producing liquid helium commercially in South Africa, which is a structural scarcity asset (global helium supply is extremely concentrated). Enrichment technology licenses are essentially un-copyable once proven — the QE/laser approach, if it scales, is a step-change in enrichment economics vs. diffusion or centrifuge.

The risks are real and front-loaded. HALEU remains pre-revenue and pre-commercial-scale — the primary investment thesis is a 2026–2030 ramp, not a current cash flow story. CEO Paul Mann took a health-related leave of absence in October 2025, with COO Robert Ainscow serving as interim CEO; Mann resumed the role in January 2026, but the episode is a governance flag at a critical execution window. The QE technology has not yet been demonstrated at the industrial HALEU scale required for SMR fuel supply — this is the core binary risk. Centrus (LEU) has US government backing and is further along on NRC licensing for its HALEU demonstration, which is a legitimate competing pathway. Regulatory permitting for US-based HALEU enrichment operations will be a multi-year process regardless of technology readiness. The company is small-cap and will likely require additional capital raises to fund the HALEU facility buildout.

**Key moat:** Exclusive global rights to ASP + QE (laser) enrichment; NECSA sovereign partnership in South Africa; no Western HALEU competitor in commercial production; Renergen helium asset.

**Key risks:** Pre-revenue on primary thesis (HALEU); QE technology scale-up risk at industrial HALEU volumes; CEO health governance flag (Oct 2025 leave, resumed Jan 2026); Centrus/LEU competing HALEU path with DOE backing; NRC regulatory timeline for US enrichment; capital raise dilution risk; small-cap liquidity.
