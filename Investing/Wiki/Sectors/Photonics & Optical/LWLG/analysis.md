## One-Line Thesis

Lightwave Logic's Perkinite electro-optic polymer is a potential step-function improvement in optical modulator efficiency for co-packaged optics — pre-revenue, high scientific merit, but pure binary execution risk on materials commercialization.

## Investment Thesis

The physics underlying Lightwave Logic's Perkinite platform is genuinely compelling. In optical transceivers and silicon photonics systems, the electro-optic modulator is the component that converts an electrical signal to a light signal — the speed and efficiency of this conversion determines bandwidth and power consumption. Lithium niobate (LiNbO3), the current leading material for high-performance modulators, achieves electro-optic coefficients of ~30 pm/V, requiring drive voltages of 5-6 volts. Perkinite achieves >200 pm/V — 6-7x higher — enabling drive voltages below 0.5V. At data center scale, where each optical link burns milliwatts and there are millions of links, the power savings are not incremental but transformational.

**Why co-packaged optics (CPO) is the inflection point:** CPO integrates optical transceivers directly with switch ASICs on the same package, eliminating pluggable transceiver power losses. CPO is the architecture chosen by TSMC (COUPE program), Intel, and hyperscalers for next-generation AI cluster interconnect. But CPO modulators must be driven at CMOS-compatible voltages (sub-1V) to avoid separate voltage level-shifter circuits. LiNbO3 at 5-6V cannot be driven by CMOS directly — requiring additional circuitry. Perkinite at <0.5V could eliminate this problem entirely, enabling the simplest possible CPO modulator implementation.

**The commercialization gap:** The physics is proven in laboratory settings. What remains unproven is: (1) CMOS-foundry-compatible deposition (Perkinite must be spin-coated onto wafers and baked — process qualification at TSMC/GlobalFoundries is required); (2) long-term reliability in field conditions (optical modulators experience continuous RF drive signals — polymer long-term stability must be verified); (3) manufacturing yield at wafer scale. These are well-defined engineering problems, not scientific unknowns, but they are expensive and time-consuming to solve.
