## One-Line Thesis

RoboStrategy (Nasdaq: BOT) is a newly-listed closed-end fund holding private stakes in pre-IPO humanoid robotics companies — primarily Figure AI — whose stock has traded at 3–5x its reported NAV, making it one of the most premium-laden and speculative vehicles in the robotics theme.

## Investment Thesis

RoboStrategy listed on Nasdaq on May 11, 2026 as a closed-end fund (not an ETF) designed to give public investors access to private, pre-IPO robotics and Physical AI companies that are otherwise venture-only. Its anchor holding is Figure AI (last valued at ~$39B), alongside Apptronik, Dyna Robotics, Standard Bots, and Dexmate. As of March 31, 2026, reported NAV was approximately $7.31–$7.34/share on total net assets of ~$145.5M. Shortly after listing the stock traded near $37–$38/share — roughly a 5x premium to NAV — implying an effective valuation of Figure's position at over $200B versus the ~$39B private-market mark.

The fund is explicitly modeled on Strategy's (MSTR) Bitcoin capital flywheel. Under this R.I.S.E. model (Raise-Invest-Scale-Expand), BOT issues new shares at a large premium to NAV, uses the proceeds to buy additional private robotics equity at private-market prices, and argues this is NAV-accretive rather than dilutive because each share issued at 3–5x NAV brings in more dollars per share than the current book value. In May 2026 BOT secured a committed equity facility (CEF) of up to $2 billion from Roth Principal Investments (subsidiary of Roth Capital Partners), giving it the ability — but not the obligation — to issue and sell common stock to Roth at its discretion over 36 months. This is structurally an ATM mechanism with a single counterparty; it is not a bank credit line.

The core controversy is straightforward: the premium to NAV is entirely sentiment-driven. Private company valuations are inherently subjective and are marked infrequently. If Figure or another holding faces a down round, NAV compresses immediately while the public stock may not reprice until sentiment shifts — creating asymmetric downside. The MSTR analogy is imperfect: Bitcoin is liquid and continuously priced; humanoid robotics stakes are illiquid and subject to write-downs. The $2B CEF creates a dilution overhang at all times. Retail buyers paying 3–5x NAV are, in effect, paying venture valuations with additional layers of management cost and zero governance input over the underlying companies.

> **Thesis drift block:** This is a pre-revenue, pre-liquidity-event speculation on the humanoid robotics cycle. The fund structure itself is the risk as much as the holdings. Any re-rating of private AI/robotics valuations (down rounds, delayed commercialization) would likely collapse the premium before collapsing NAV.

**Key moat:** First-mover as a public closed-end fund in the humanoid robotics theme; MSTR brand-association driving retail premiums; $2B CEF provides capital to compound positions if the premium holds.

**Key risks:** Extreme premium to NAV (3–5x at listing) with no intrinsic floor; illiquid underlying holdings subject to subjective marks; heavy dilution mechanism via Roth CEF; CEO (Andrew Kang) comes from crypto/DeFi with no established robotics operating track record; closed-end fund structure means discount-to-NAV is equally possible if sentiment reverses; no dividend, no redemption mechanism for shareholders.
