## Investment Thesis

Zebra Technologies is the dominant infrastructure provider for physical operations visibility. The company's roughly 10,700-person organization supplies barcode scanners, mobile computers, thermal printers, RFID readers, and machine vision systems to warehouses, retail floors, hospitals, and manufacturing plants worldwide. Its 2014 acquisition of Motorola Solutions' Enterprise Business tripled its scale and locked in a massive installed base that drives recurring replacement cycles and software attach revenue.

The business model compounds through high switching costs and ecosystem depth. Enterprises standardize on Zebra hardware because integrations with WMS, ERP, and EHR systems are deep and re-platforming is operationally disruptive. Zebra estimates it holds roughly 42% market share in global enterprise mobile computing — a share that translates into pricing power and predictable refresh demand every 3–5 years. Services and software reached approximately 19% of $5.4 billion in 2025 revenue, and management targets a low-double-digit recurring revenue mix by 2026, which would meaningfully improve margin quality.

The company made a significant strategic correction in late 2025, exiting its Fetch Robotics-derived AMR business after the unit failed to scale. Zebra sold the Fetch assets to Skild AI and will record up to $80 million in one-time charges. The exit frees capital and management attention to focus on Zebra's genuine strengths: connected frontline computing and automated visibility via RFID. The new segment structure — Connected Frontline (CF) and Automated Visibility & Analytics (AVA) — reflects this refocused strategy. Q1 2025 organic net sales grew 11.9% year-over-year (CF +8.6%, AVA +18.4%), with RFID accelerating as enterprises seek inventory accuracy to combat shrink and out-of-stocks.

**Key moat:** 42% share in enterprise mobile computing; deep WMS/ERP integrations driving 3–5 year replacement cycles with high switching costs; RFID consumables and services creating recurring revenue streams on top of a multi-decade installed base.

**Key risks:** Enterprise IT capex cycles are lumpy — Zebra suffered a sharp inventory correction in 2023–2024 when customers digested pandemic over-ordering; the AMR exit signals a costly failed diversification that raises questions about future M&A discipline; competition from Honeywell in rugged devices and from emerging Chinese hardware vendors on price; software attach rate targets remain aspirational with slow execution to date.

## Management & Leadership

| Role | Name | Tenure | Background |
|------|------|--------|------------|
| CEO | Bill Burns | Since March 2023 | Joined Zebra 2015 as Chief Product & Solutions Officer; prior CEO of Embrane (acquired by Cisco) and Spirent Communications; 30+ years in enterprise technology |
| CFO | Nathan Winters | Since January 2021 | Joined Zebra 2018; previously VP of Corporate FP&A and Business Operations at Zebra; 20+ years in finance |

**Execution track record:** Burns oversaw product strategy during Zebra's post-Motorola integration and navigated the 2023–2024 inventory correction with aggressive cost discipline, returning nearly $600 million to shareholders via buybacks in 2025. The Fetch Robotics exit, while a write-off, demonstrates willingness to cut losses on failed bets and refocus on core strengths. Winters tightened working capital management through the downcycle and maintained investment-grade balance sheet flexibility.

**Insider ownership / alignment:** Stock ownership guidelines require the CEO to hold 6x base salary in ZBRA shares, other named executives 4x, and Section 16 officers 3x, tying leadership wealth to long-term stock performance. Institutional ownership is concentrated — Vanguard holds approximately 7.36% — with insider ownership relatively modest, consistent with a mature large-cap. The 2026 proxy introduced a new LTIP structure, signaling a refresh of long-term incentive alignment.
