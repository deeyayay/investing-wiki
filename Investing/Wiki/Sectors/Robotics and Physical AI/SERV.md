## Investment Thesis

Serve Robotics builds Level 4 autonomous sidewalk delivery robots and deploys them as a managed fleet service for major food-delivery platforms. The company spun out of Postmates (acquired by Uber) and holds multi-year deployment agreements with Uber Eats (up to 2,000 robots) and DoorDash, giving it a locked-in distribution channel that pure-hardware competitors lack. The moment matters now because last-mile delivery costs remain the structural ceiling on food-delivery unit economics — a sub-$1 robot delivery vs. an $8–10 human courier trip is a compelling wedge, and Serve is the only publicly traded pure-play on sidewalk autonomy at commercial scale.

The bull case is straightforward unit-economics math: at 2,000+ deployed robots with high utilization, each incremental order carries near-zero marginal cost. Management guided $26M revenue for 2026 — approximately 10× the FY2025 base of $2.7M — underpinned by contracted deployments rather than speculative pipeline. The company also acquired Phantom Auto (ultra-low-latency teleoperation) and Vayu (AI foundation model autonomy), which should compress data-infrastructure costs and reduce reliance on human oversight over time. A secondary revenue layer — in-robot branding and advertising — adds upside without requiring additional hardware.

The moat is layered: years of real-world sidewalk miles create a proprietary autonomy dataset that is very hard to replicate quickly; exclusive multi-year platform contracts create switching friction; and the indoor-autonomy extension via Moxi hospital robots suggests the core stack can expand across verticals, widening the data flywheel further. With >$200M in liquidity as of early 2026, Serve has runway to reach the utilization thresholds where unit economics turn favorable.

Honest risks: Serve is pre-profitability and burning cash to scale. Revenue is almost entirely contract-dependent — losing or not renewing the Uber Eats agreement would be severe. The robotics-deployment thesis requires sustained regulatory permitting in city-by-city markets, which is slow and unpredictable. NVIDIA, formerly a ~10% anchor shareholder and key credibility signal, exited its stake by end of 2024, removing a visible strategic endorsement. Dilution risk is material given the growth-financing posture.

**Key moat:** Multi-year exclusive platform contracts (Uber Eats, DoorDash) combined with a proprietary sidewalk-autonomy dataset accumulated from commercial deployments at scale — difficult and time-consuming for new entrants to replicate.

**Key risks:** Pre-profitability cash burn; single-customer revenue concentration; city-by-city regulatory exposure; NVIDIA has fully exited its equity stake.

**Execution track record:** Kashani led the company from Postmates spin-out to Nasdaq listing and scaled the fleet from pilot to 1,000+ deployed units by Q3 2025, on track for 2,000 by year-end. Read brings disciplined public-company financial controls from prior controller roles at two Nasdaq-listed hardware companies.

**Insider ownership / alignment:** Per the FY2025 DEF 14A (filed 2025), Postmates LLC (Uber affiliate) is a >5% beneficial owner; exact executive insider ownership percentages are not broken out in public summaries but management holds equity aligned with the deployment scale-up milestones. NVIDIA held approximately 10% (3.73M shares) as of mid-2024 but sold its entire stake by end of 2024 — that strategic endorsement is no longer reflected in the cap table.
