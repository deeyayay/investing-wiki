## Investment Thesis

Stryker is the dominant vertically integrated orthopedic medical device company, with leading market share in joint replacement, surgical equipment, neurotechnology, and spine. The Mako robotic system — now deployed across more than 3,000 hospitals globally — sits at the center of the thesis: once a hospital installs Mako, Stryker captures the downstream implant revenue for every knee, hip, and shoulder procedure that robot performs. That razor/blade dynamic, combined with multi-year capital budgeting cycles, creates a revenue stream competitors cannot easily dislodge.

The bull case rests on three compounding drivers. First, robotic joint replacement adoption remains early; total knee robotics penetration in the U.S. is still well below 50%, providing a long runway as Mako expands into hips, shoulders, and spine. Second, the platform generates structured intraoperative data — kinematics, alignment, outcomes — that feeds back into surgeon training and AI-assisted guidance, widening the performance gap versus mechanical technique and making each new Mako 4 installation stickier than the last. Third, Stryker's scale allows consistent 100+ bps annual adjusted operating margin expansion even at 10%+ organic growth, validating the operating leverage embedded in the model.

Full-year 2025 net sales reached $25.1 billion (+11.2% reported, +10.3% organic), and adjusted EPS grew 11.8% to $13.63. Management guided 2026 organic growth of 8.0–9.5% and adjusted EPS of $14.90–$15.10, a slight deceleration but still among the highest growth rates in large-cap medtech. Q1 2026 results came in short of consensus, partly due to a March 2026 cybersecurity incident and $118 million in restructuring charges; these are transient, not structural.

**Key moat:** Installed Mako base creates razor/blade implant pull-through with high switching costs; proprietary intraoperative data flywheel deepens clinical differentiation over time; scale across five distinct product segments insulates against any single market softening.

**Key risks:** Competitive robotics entrants (Smith & Nephew Cori, Zimmer Biomet ROSA) eroding new system placements; cybersecurity incidents disrupting hospital operations and compressing near-term margins; tariff and supply-chain exposure on imported components; valuation premium (~30× forward earnings) leaves limited margin of safety if organic growth decelerates materially.
