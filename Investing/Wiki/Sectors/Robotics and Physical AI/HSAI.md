## Investment Thesis

Hesai Group designs and manufactures LiDAR sensors for two structurally growing end markets: mass-market ADAS in passenger and commercial vehicles, and high-performance robotics including humanoids, robotaxis, and delivery drones. Founded in 2014 and listed on NASDAQ in 2024, Hesai crossed profitability in 2024 before any Western LiDAR peer — validating a business model built on relentless cost reduction rather than venture-subsidized growth. The company shipped over 1 million units in 2025, a milestone that triggers a self-reinforcing cycle: scale drives margin expansion, which funds next-generation chip and sensor R&D, which wins the next wave of design contracts.

The bull case rests on two compounding drivers. First, ADAS adoption in China is accelerating ahead of Western markets, and Hesai holds dominant domestic share with design wins at BYD, Li Auto, and other high-volume OEMs; 2026 guidance calls for 2–3 million ADAS unit shipments, implying continued volume-led margin leverage. Second, the robotics TAM is described by management as "several times larger than ADAS" — and Hesai is moving early: it secured an ~10 million unit exclusive LiDAR supply agreement with Dreame and MOVA for robotic lawn mowers, and is embedded in humanoid robot and robotaxi programs globally, including a Toyota ADAS design win targeting mass production in 2026.

Hesai's moat is manufacturing scale combined with proprietary ASIC and MEMS-based chip design. Building custom silicon in-house collapses per-unit cost at pace that fabless competitors cannot match, and each successive volume ramp widens the gap. The dual-class share structure keeps founding engineers in control, reducing the risk of strategy drift under Wall Street pressure. Overseas expansion (Europe, Japan, Southeast Asia) is the next frontier — early traction with Toyota is a proof point, though the timeline to meaningful international revenue remains uncertain.

Key risks are real and specific: U.S.–China trade tensions and potential export controls or NDAA-style blacklisting could impair access to U.S. customers and EDA tooling. Domestic Chinese competition from RoboSense and Innoviz is intensifying. Camera-only and radar-only autonomy architectures remain an existential alternative, particularly as Tesla and others push sensor-fusion approaches that exclude LiDAR. Margin sustainability at scale has not been proven through a full economic cycle, and the company remains exposed to single-geography concentration risk.

**Key moat:** Vertically integrated ASIC/MEMS chip design enabling a structural cost-per-unit advantage that widens with each manufacturing scale step.

**Key risks:** U.S.–China geopolitical exposure (blacklist / export control risk); camera-only autonomy architectures displacing LiDAR; domestic Chinese competition from RoboSense; single-geography revenue concentration.

**Execution track record:** Hesai reached profitability ahead of every Western LiDAR peer and scaled from zero to 1 million+ annual unit shipments in roughly a decade while maintaining positive gross margins of ~42% — a rare combination of engineering execution and financial discipline in a sector known for cash burn.

**Insider ownership / alignment:** Co-founders Li Yifan, Shaoqing Xiang (CTO), and Kai Sun (Chief Scientist) each hold approximately 6.8–7.1% of shares outstanding; founders maintain majority voting control via a dual-class share structure.
