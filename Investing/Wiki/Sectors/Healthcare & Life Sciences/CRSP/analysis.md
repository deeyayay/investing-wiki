## Investment Thesis

> **Thesis established:** 2026-06-22
> **Last validated:** 2026-06-22
> **Drift status:** Early commercial — Casgevy $116M FY2025 revenue; >500 patients initiated; $2.44B liquidity (Q1 2026); CEO: 2026 "a defining year"

CRISPR Therapeutics is the only company with a commercially approved CRISPR gene editing therapy. Casgevy (exa-cel, co-developed with Vertex Pharmaceuticals) received FDA and EMA approval in late 2023 for sickle cell disease (SCD) and transfusion-dependent beta-thalassemia (TDT) — collectively affecting ~30,000 patients in the US and EU who are eligible for the one-time curative treatment. Casgevy generated $116M in revenue in FY2025, reflecting an early-commercial trajectory constrained by manufacturing throughput, treatment center capacity, and the ~9-12 month patient preparation timeline inherent to ex-vivo gene therapy. More than 500 patients have initiated the Casgevy treatment process, and the annual run-rate accelerated through Q4 2025. The structural bull case is that a one-time cure for a severe genetic disease commands both high pricing power (Casgevy is priced at $2.2M per patient) and near-zero patient churn by definition.

The partnership structure with Vertex (40% CRSP / 60% Vertex on Casgevy profits) means Vertex bears the commercial execution weight: Vertex's sales force, authorized treatment center network, and reimbursement infrastructure handle market access. This de-risks commercialization for CRSP but also caps near-term economics. CRSP's leverage in the relationship is that it contributes the CRISPR IP and clinical development capabilities — and retains more favorable economics if it can execute independently in future programs. The $2.44B cash position (Q1 2026, boosted by $585M in convertible notes) funds operations for at least 24 months and funds the pipeline through key milestones.

Beyond Casgevy, the most watched pipeline asset is CTX112: an allogeneic (off-the-shelf) CAR-T for B-cell malignancies, in Phase 1. Allogeneic CAR-T eliminates the manufacturing bottleneck of autologous approaches (where cells are extracted from the patient) — if CTX112 demonstrates comparable efficacy to autologous therapies, the commercial addressable market expands dramatically by removing the 4-6 week manufacturing wait. CRISPR Therapeutics is also advancing in-vivo CRISPR programs (editing genes directly in the body without cell extraction) for cardiovascular and liver diseases, which represent a longer-duration but potentially larger opportunity. Autoimmune programs (CTX310, CTX320) are in early trials.

Key risks: the 40/60 Vertex profit share means CRSP will not turn operating-positive on Casgevy alone for several years — the $582M net loss in 2025 reflects both the profit share arrangement and continued R&D investment. Casgevy patient activation pace is slower than some expected due to treatment center preparation timelines and insurance prior authorization complexity. In-vivo CRISPR safety — off-target edits causing unintended DNA changes — remains an industry-wide concern that could impair the platform's future expansion if adverse events emerge. Base editing (BEAM) and prime editing competitors may offer higher-precision alternatives in overlapping indications.

**Key moat:** First commercially approved CRISPR therapy + foundational IP position + Vertex commercialization infrastructure; >500 patients initiated creates real-world evidence base; $2.4B cash runway covers pipeline through defining milestones.

**Key risks:** Casgevy patient ramp slower than modeled; 40/60 Vertex profit share limits near-term CRSP economics; net loss $582M in 2025; off-target editing safety risk could affect the platform; allogeneic CAR-T is unproven in Phase 3; Swiss HQ introduces some regulatory jurisdictional complexity.
