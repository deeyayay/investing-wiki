# Space & Comms — Sector Framework

_Created: 2026-05-18 | Review quarterly or after major sector events_

## Why This Sector Exists in the Portfolio

Space has transitioned from a government-monopoly, 20-year program cycle to a commercially-driven, 3-year launch cadence. The enabling factor was cost reduction: SpaceX's Falcon 9 reduced orbital launch costs by 10x. That cost reduction unlocked constellations that were economically impossible in the prior era — ASTS's direct-to-cell coverage, Starlink's global broadband, and the commercial satellite imagery industry now all depend on sub-$5,000/kg launch economics.

The two names in this portfolio represent distinct but complementary theses: **RKLB (Rocket Lab) is the launch and space systems infrastructure play** — the picks-and-shovels of the new space economy. **ASTS (AST SpaceMobile) is the connectivity application play** — a bet that direct-to-cell broadband can capture a billion-dollar recurring revenue stream from mobile network operators who are contractually obligated to pay for coverage regardless of subscriber count.

The investment thesis is not "space is exciting." It's: **launch frequency and satellite deployment are structural demand curves with no ceiling in sight, and the infrastructure to support them is only being built by a handful of companies.**

---

## How Value Is Created in This Sector

**1. Launch economics and frequency as a compounding moat.** Rocket Lab's Electron is the world's most frequently launched small rocket. Each launch builds operational data, manufacturing process refinement, and customer trust. The learning curve in rocketry is steep — companies that launch frequently get better faster. RKLB's moat is not the rocket hardware; it's the operational cadence, range infrastructure, and systems expertise that compounds with each mission.

**2. Space systems revenue as a durable recurring business.** RKLB's Space Systems segment (satellites, star trackers, reaction wheels, solar panels, software) is growing faster than launch and carries higher margins. This is the segment that can scale without per-mission rocket risk. Every satellite constellation deployed by a third party is a potential Space Systems customer.

**3. Direct-to-cell satellite connectivity as a new access layer.** ASTS is building a geostationary-scale broadband constellation in LEO that can communicate directly with standard unmodified mobile handsets. If it works at scale, every mobile carrier in the world has an economic incentive to become an ASTS customer — because ASTS coverage eliminates "dead zones" that MNOs contractually guarantee to cover but currently cannot. The product is wholesale broadband; carriers pay per unit of capacity sold.

**4. Government and defense recurring contracts.** Both RKLB and ASTS have meaningful government revenue streams (DoD, NASA, NRO). Defense contracts provide revenue stability that offsets commercial launch/connectivity cyclicality. RKLB's HASTE hypersonic testbed and government small satellite buses are examples — they don't generate venture-scale returns, but they're high-quality recurring cash flow.

## Industry Structure — Value Chain Map

| Layer | Role | Key Players | Moat Type | Margin Profile |
|-------|------|-------------|-----------|----------------|
| **Heavy Launch** | Large payload to orbit | SpaceX, ULA, Ariane | Scale + reuse technology | Medium, recovering |
| **Small Launch** | Small/medium payload, responsive cadence | RKLB, ABL, Firefly | Launch frequency + range ops | Medium, improving |
| **Space Systems** | Satellite buses, components, software | RKLB, Maxar, Astroscale | Integration IP + product breadth | Medium-high |
| **Satellite Connectivity** | Broadband/IoT services from orbit | ASTS, SpaceX (Starlink), Iridium | Orbital frequency + MNO contracts | Very high potential |
| **Earth Observation** | Commercial imagery and data analytics | Planet, Spire, BlackSky | Data frequency + analytics layer | Medium |
| **Ground Systems** | Satellite command, communication, data relay | General Dynamics, AWS Ground Station | Customer lock-in + security clearances | Medium, recurring |

**Structural insight:** The highest-value positions are at the points of hardest replication: launch cadence (RKLB — only handful of companies globally can execute reliable small launch) and direct-to-cell coverage (ASTS — the technical barrier to building a constellation that works with unmodified phones is extremely high). Both companies are 3–5 year head starts ahead of credible competition.

## Company Archetypes and How to Evaluate Them

### Archetype 1: Launch + Space Systems Platform (RKLB)

_Vertical integration across launch and satellite systems — the space economy infrastructure provider_

**What matters most:** Launch cadence (quarterly manifest — is Electron flying at or above 15/year?), Space Systems revenue growth and gross margin (this is where the operating leverage lives), Neutron development timeline (medium-lift rocket — the 2027–2028 addressable market expansion story), backlog conversion rate (signed contracts → revenue recognition pace), government contract win rate (classified missions signal capability credibility).

**RKLB-specific dynamics:** Rocket Lab is unique in being both a launch provider and a full satellite manufacturer. The satellite component business (reaction wheels, star trackers, solar panels) is sold to competitors' satellites — RKLB is picks-and-shovels for the entire LEO constellation buildout. Monitor: does component revenue diversification reduce launch revenue cyclicality? It should over time.

**Neutron is the optionality:** A successful Neutron rocket (targeting ~13-ton payload, reusable) would put RKLB in direct competition with Falcon 9 for medium-lift commercial and government missions. This is a 2–3x revenue expansion scenario if execution is on track. Watch: engine development milestones, Wallops Island launch pad construction progress.

**Valuation:** EV/Revenue during the pre-profitability phase (2–5x for a company at this growth trajectory and sector). Transition to EV/EBITDA once Space Systems reaches consistent profitability. The Neutron timeline compresses the path to profitability — model it as a separate scenario rather than embedding it in base case.

**Warning signs:** Electron mission failure (loss of vehicle / customer payload — two failures in 12 months would signal systemic issue), Neutron development delays pushing past 2029, government contract losses to competitors (launch, not just components), Space Systems gross margin declining below 25%.

### Archetype 2: Direct-to-Cell Satellite Connectivity (ASTS)

_High-risk, high-reward bet on mobile broadband from space — size for optionality, not certainty_

**What matters most:** Satellite deployment cadence (BlueBird Block 1/2/3 — how many cells in orbit defines coverage capability), MNO commercial contract conversions (AT&T, Verizon, Vodafone, Rakuten LOIs → binding revenue commitments), ARPU per active coverage zone (the unit economics proof point), satellite manufacturing cost per unit (declining cost drives the path to positive unit economics), in-orbit performance data (actual link budget, throughput, handoff performance in field tests).

**ASTS-specific dynamics:** ASTS's business model is wholesale — it sells connectivity capacity to mobile network operators, not directly to consumers. This is intentional: MNOs do the retail distribution, customer service, and billing. ASTS collects per-MB or per-session wholesale fees. The contracted MNO base (AT&T, Verizon, Rakuten, dozens of others) represents a revenue floor that is committed regardless of end-consumer adoption.

The technical risk is real: operating a cellular base station from a 700km orbit while maintaining 3GPP compliance with unmodified handsets requires extraordinary engineering. The commercial risk is equally real: if throughput per satellite is insufficient to be economically competitive with terrestrial coverage expansion, MNOs will not renew at full rates.

**Valuation:** Probability-weighted scenario analysis. In the bull case (20B+ addressable subs, $2–5/month ARPU on 5% penetration), ASTS's revenue could be $2–10B annually — which supports a $30–100/share valuation range. In the bear case (technical limitations cap throughput, MNOs don't renew beyond initial commitments), ASTS approaches zero. Position size must reflect the full-loss bear case.

**Warning signs:** BlueBird satellites failing to meet link budget targets in LEO testing, MNO commercial contract negotiations stalling or repricing lower, competing direct-to-cell services (SpaceX Starlink Direct-to-Cell, T-Mobile) gaining meaningful market share, cash burn accelerating past $200M/quarter without proportional deployment progress.

## Where We Are in the Industry Cycle

**Current phase: Commercial infrastructure buildout (2025–2028)**

Small launch is transitioning from development phase to operational scale. RKLB is executing at 10–14 launches per year; the target is 20+/year as Electron manufacturing scales. The market is growing faster than supply: US government alone requires 100+ small satellite launches per year by 2027.

ASTS is in the deployment phase — the first commercial BlueBird constellation is going up, initial MNO deals are being formalized, and the key technical validation (does it actually work with unmodified phones at scale?) is being answered in real-time.

**Signals that mark the inflection:**
- RKLB: Neutron completing a first successful orbital test (defines the medium-lift market opportunity)
- ASTS: First MNO commercial billing statement (revenue recognition from a contracted wholesale deal)
- Industry: SpaceX Starship achieving consistent orbital cadence (changes heavy-lift economics and puts pressure on medium-lift pricing)

**The 2028–2030 horizon:** RKLB should be approaching cash flow breakeven on Space Systems alone, with Neutron providing revenue upside. ASTS either has a functioning constellation generating $500M+ ARR or has experienced a reset that requires reassessment of the thesis.

## Sector Bull / Base / Bear Cases

**Bull case (40%):** RKLB Neutron launches successfully in 2027, doubling the addressable launch market. Space Systems revenue crosses $500M annually by 2027 at 35%+ gross margin. ASTS BlueBird Block 2 deployment completes on schedule; AT&T and Verizon commercial contracts go live in 2026 with $100M+ ARR commitments. Direct-to-cell becomes the standard MNO coverage supplement globally.

**Base case (45%):** RKLB grows Electron to 18 launches/year by 2027; Space Systems revenue reaches $400M at 28–32% gross margin. Neutron debut in 2028. ASTS achieves initial MNO commercial revenues of $50–150M ARR by end-2027 with continued constellation buildout in progress. Both names pre-profitable on an operating segment basis.

**Bear case (15%):** Electron has a mission failure that grounds the vehicle for 6+ months. Neutron development delayed to 2030. ASTS throughput limitations prove insurmountable at scale; MNO wholesale rates reset 60% lower, making the unit economics non-viable. ASTS requires a large dilutive equity raise. SpaceX Starlink Direct-to-Cell captures majority of MNO wholesale deals.
