| Compound Semiconductor & SiC Substrate | Grow gallium arsenide, indium phosphide, gallium nitride, and silicon carbide crystals and epitaxial layers for photonic, RF, and power devices that silicon cannot serve | InP substrate: LEC (liquid-encapsulated Czochralski) or VGF (vertical gradient freeze) crystal growth of InP melt + B2O3 encapsulant → 75mm or 100mm boule → wafering + CMP; GaAs substrate: VGF from GaAs melt → 100mm or 150mm boule → CMP to Ra <0.3nm; MOCVD epitaxy: trimethyl-Ga (TMGa) + AsH3 → GaAs, trimethyl-In (TMIn) + PH3 → InP lattice match, multiple QW (quantum well) layers grown to ±1Å thickness control by RHEED/reflectometry; SiC boule (PVT): SiC powder sublimation at 2200°C in Ar atmosphere → seed crystal growth → 150mm or 200mm 4H-SiC boule, growth rate ~1mm/hr → wire-saw slicing → CMP to epi-ready Ra <0.1nm; GaN-on-SiC MOCVD: AlN nucleation layer + GaN buffer + AlGaN barrier → HEMT structure; SiC epitaxy (CVD): SiH4 + C3H8 at 1600°C → n-type drift layer (10–100µm, 1–10×1015 /cm³ doping) | InP wafer (50mm, 75mm, 100mm; Fe-doped semi-insulating or S-doped n+); GaAs wafer (100mm, 150mm; SI or n+); GaN-on-SiC HEMT epi wafer (3µm GaN + 25nm AlGaN barrier); 4H-SiC substrate (150mm or 200mm, n-type, micropipe density <0.1/cm²); SiC epi wafer (10–100µm drift layer, for MOSFET or Schottky); MOCVD epitaxial wafer (InGaAsP MQW for 1310/1550nm laser) | Yes | High | Process IP + Geographic concentration (InP: AXT / Wafer Technology / Sumitomo Electric — 3 suppliers; SiC substrate: Wolfspeed ~35% global share; GaN MOCVD epi: IQE dominant in foundry model) | High |
| EDA & Design IP | Provide the electronic design automation software and licensable chip IP that allows engineers to design billion-transistor chips without understanding every process detail | RTL design (Verilog/SystemVerilog/VHDL) → behavioral simulation (VCS, Questa); logic synthesis (DC Ultra, Genus): RTL → gate-level netlist optimization (timing/area/power); place & route (IC Compiler II, Innovus): floorplan → power plan → placement → CTS (clock tree synthesis) → routing (40+ metal layers at leading nodes); static timing analysis (STA, PrimeTime): sign-off at all corners (process, voltage, temperature); DRC / LVS sign-off (Calibre, PVS): layout rule check vs. foundry PDK → physical verification; GDSII / OASIS tape-out: design database delivered to mask shop; formal verification (VC Formal, JasperGold): RTL equivalence checking; simulation: SPICE (device-level), HSPICE (transistor-level), FastSPICE (block-level); IP integration: ARM Cortex/Neoverse CPU core, PCIe 6.0 PHY, CXL 3.1 controller, DDR5/HBM3 controller, USB4 | EDA software license (Synopsys IC Compiler II, Cadence Innovus, Siemens Calibre); RTL source code; gate-level netlist; GDSII layout database; standard cell library (7nm, 5nm, 3nm, 2nm nodes); ARM Cortex-X4 / Neoverse V3 CPU IP; PCIe Gen 6.0 / CXL 3.1 PHY IP; LPDDR5X / HBM3e controller IP | Yes | Low | Switching cost + Ecosystem (PDK-tool co-optimization: Synopsys and TSMC co-develop process rule decks — switching EDA vendor requires re-validating every sign-off flow; ARM CPU ISA ecosystem lock-in is architectural) | Very High |
| Chip Design (Fabless & IDM) | Design the logic, memory, and analog circuits that define a chip's function, then optimize for performance, power, and area (PPA) at a target process node — the highest-value-add step in the semiconductor industry | Architecture definition: microarchitecture specification (ISA, cache hierarchy, memory subsystem, interconnect), power-performance trade-off modeling, die area budget; RTL coding → functional simulation → design verification (UVM testbench, coverage closure, assertion-based verification); logic synthesis and physical design (via EDA tier above); DFT (design for test) insertion: ATPG, scan chain, BIST, JTAG boundary scan; custom circuit design: SRAM bitcell (6T/8T), analog PLL (CPPLL, DPLL), ADC/DAC, SerDes analog front-end; tape-out preparation: PDK rule deck signoff, IR drop analysis, antenna effect fixes, GDSII merge; post-silicon validation: bring-up, functional debug (JTAG/JTAG-DP), parametric characterization, yield learning loop; custom ASIC: joint architecture (hyperscaler + chip company), PPA target negotiation, NRE funding model | AI GPU (NVDA H200 / B200, AMD MI300X) — billion-transistor, HBM3e-integrated, TSMC N4P/N3E; custom ASIC (Google TPU v5, AWS Trainium2, Meta MTIA) — transformer-optimized, CoWoS packaged; CPU (AMD EPYC Turin, Intel Xeon 6) — server compute; networking ASIC (Broadcom Tomahawk 5, Marvell Teralynx) — 51.2T switching; SerDes IP (Marvell PAM4, Credo Ultra-Short Reach) — 112G / 224G electrical lane; power semiconductor chip (SiC MOSFET, GaN HEMT, IGBT) | Yes | Low–Medium (design) / Very High (fabs used) | Ecosystem (CUDA: 4M+ developer ecosystem; ARM ISA: 95%+ mobile, growing server; custom ASIC: switching cost is full re-architecture at $100M+ NRE) | Very High |
| Interconnect & SerDes Silicon | Design the retimers, SerDes PHYs, and PCIe/CXL/Ethernet connectivity chips that move data between accelerators, servers, and racks at 112G/224G per lane — the electrical signaling layer that lets compute and memory talk | SerDes PHY design (112G/224G PAM4, DSP-based equalization, FEC); retimer / redriver (signal conditioning across long PCB traces and in-rack cables); PCIe Gen6 / CXL 3.x controller + PHY; active electrical cable (AEC) for in-rack copper; UCIe die-to-die PHY for chiplets; package co-design for signal/power integrity | PCIe/CXL retimer (Astera Aries); 224G ultra-short-reach SerDes (Credo); active electrical cable (AEC); chiplet die-to-die PHY (UCIe); Ethernet SerDes IP | Partial | Low–Medium | Process IP + Switching cost (224G mixed-signal SerDes is a deep analog/DSP specialty; design-in cycles lock customers for a full GPU generation) | Very High |
| HBM (High-Bandwidth Memory) | Stack DRAM die vertically and mount them on-package beside the GPU to deliver the extreme bandwidth that keeps an AI accelerator fed — the bandwidth tier of the hierarchy | HBM stacking: TSV (through-silicon via) drilling + microbump or Cu-Cu hybrid bonding → 8-/12-/16-hi DRAM stack on a base logic die → known-good-stack (KGS) test; on-package integration: HBM join to GPU via CoWoS interposer | HBM3e / HBM4 stack (12-hi, 36GB, ~1.2 TB/s, on-package KV-cache memory); HBM base logic die | Yes | Very High | Scale + Process IP (three-supplier oligopoly — SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron; output pre-allocated and CoWoS-packaging-gated) | High (cyclical) |

| NAND Flash (Storage) | Fabricate non-volatile 3D-stacked flash — the SSD and enterprise storage that holds model weights, datasets, and checkpoints — the cheapest, densest capacity tier | NAND: 3D charge-trap vertical channel stacking (200+ layers, TLC/QLC) → string/array test → wafer bond to CMOS periphery (CBA); SSD controller + module assembly | QLC enterprise NAND / NVMe SSD; client SSD; UFS storage | Partial | Very High | Scale + Process IP (layer-count and cost-per-bit race; capital-cycle commodity) | Low–Medium (cyclical) |
| LPDDR (Low-Power & Edge Memory) | Fabricate low-power DRAM optimized for power efficiency over raw bandwidth — the working memory for mobile, automotive, edge inference, and low-power AI servers (e.g., Grace) | DRAM cell fab (shared with system DRAM) → LPDDR5X array; low-power module assembly (SOCAMM, LPCAMM2) | LPDDR5X DRAM; SOCAMM / LPCAMM2 low-power modules | No | Very High | Scale + Process IP (same fabs as system DRAM; packaging/spec differentiation) | Medium (cyclical) |
| Lithography Equipment | Project photomask circuit patterns onto photoresist-coated wafers at nanometer precision using UV or EUV light — the single most critical and concentrated manufacturing equipment tier | EUV light generation: 50kW CO2 laser pulse → tin (Sn) droplet → plasma at 50,000K → 13.5nm EUV photons (1–2% conversion efficiency) → ellipsoidal collector mirror; multilayer optics: Mo/Si multilayer mirror (40 pairs, λ/4 thickness, >67% EUV reflectivity per mirror) → 6-mirror projection system; wafer stage: dual-wafer stage (expose + measure in parallel), magnetic levitation, 0.25nm positioning accuracy; DUV scanner (ArF immersion): 193nm laser + water immersion → 0.33 NA → 38nm single-pass feature; multiple patterning: SAQP (self-aligned quadruple patterning) for <10nm lines without EUV; EUV pellicle: polysilicon membrane (50nm thick, >90% EUV transmission, placed over reticle) → prevents particle contamination on mask; high-NA EUV (0.55 NA): anamorphic 8-mirror system, field size 26×16.5mm → <2nm feature capability | EUV scanner (ASML NXE:3800E, 0.33 NA, 170 WPH, EUV power 250W); High-NA EUV scanner (ASML EXE:5000, 0.55 NA, target 2025 HVM); DUV ArF immersion scanner (ASML TWINSCAN NXT:2100i, 0.33 NA, 295 WPH); EUV photomask (quartz + TaN absorber + EUV multilayer; manufactured by Toppan, Dai Nippon, Photronics); EUV pellicle (RSLi polysilicon, ~50nm, >90% transmission); reticle pod (EUV SMIF pod, Class 1 particle cleanliness) | Yes | Very High | Process IP + Geographic monopoly (ASML is the sole supplier of EUV scanners globally; High-NA EUV is a sole-source monopoly; DUV: ASML + Nikon + Canon, ASML dominant) | Very High |
| Deposition & Etch Equipment | Deposit thin-film layers atom-by-atom and etch features with sub-angstrom control to build transistors, interconnects, and dielectrics across 100+ process steps | ALD (atomic layer deposition): self-limiting surface reaction → TMA + H2O → Al2O3 (high-K gate dielectric, 1nm/cycle); PEALD: plasma-enhanced ALD for low-temperature oxides (PECVD SiNx, SiO2 at <400°C); CVD (chemical vapor deposition): WF6 + SiH4/H2 → W plug fill, SiH4 + N2O → TEOS oxide ILD; PVD/sputtering: magnetron sputter of TiN barrier, Cu seed, Ta/TaN liner for damascene; plasma etch: CCP (capacitively-coupled plasma) or ICP (inductively-coupled) → selective Si, oxide, nitride, metal etch; ALE (atomic layer etch): self-limiting modification (Cl2 passivation) + directional ion removal → sub-1nm etch control for gate recess; wet clean: SC-1, DHF, SPM strip → remove etch residues and particles between steps; EUV hardmask etch: TiN hardmask open, SiN spacer etch for SAQP | CVD/ALD chamber (LRCX VECTOR, AMAT Centura, TEL Trias); high-aspect-ratio contact etch tool (CCP, ER > 50:1); damascene Cu plating tool (Lam SABRE 3D, ECD); PVD chamber (AMAT Endura); TiN ALD (TiCl4 + NH3, barrier for Cu/W); High-K gate dielectric (HfO2, ~1nm EOT, ALD); W plug fill (WF6 + SiH4 CVD); Cu interconnect (ECD plating + CMP, 10nm min pitch at N3) | Partial | High | Process IP + Switching cost (process recipes are co-developed with foundry; tool-foundry qualification takes 12–18 months; mid-node tool swap is impractical) | High |
| Inspection & Metrology | Measure every critical dimension and detect every printable defect in real time — the feedback loop that enables yield learning and process control | Broadband plasma optical inspection: bright-field (reflected illumination → defect scattered light collection) + dark-field (grazing illumination → particles scatter) → 300mm wafer map at 10–20nm defect sensitivity; e-beam inspection (EBI): SEM-based → higher resolution, lower throughput → used at every new node for defect characterization; CD-SEM (critical dimension SEM): FESEM → measure critical dimension (CD) of gate, spacer, contact → 0.1nm repeatability; OCD (optical critical dimension / scatterometry): reflectance spectrum fitting → extract CD, sidewall angle, layer thickness from grating targets (non-destructive); overlay metrology: moiré-based (ASML YieldStar) or e-beam → measure layer-to-layer alignment error (<0.5nm allowed at N3); XRF / XRR: X-ray fluorescence + reflectometry → measure thin-film composition and thickness; CDSEM recipe management: automated defect classification (ADC) → ML-based defect binning → DCP (defect-cause-prevention) | Broadband plasma optical inspection tool (KLA Surfscan SP7, defect sensitivity to 19nm); e-beam inspection (KLA eSL10, EDU system); CD-SEM (Applied HorizonXT, Hitachi CG7300); optical overlay metrology (ASML YieldStar T-1000D, KLA Archer); inline OCD scatterometry (KLA Aleris, Nova VeraFlex); XRF film gauge (Hitachi XRF SFT-9550); process control software (KLA KT-Analyzer, Onto Stratus) | Partial | Medium | Switching cost + Certification (inspection recipe qualification is 6–12 months; process control data streams integrate deeply into fab MES — switching tool vendor disrupts entire yield learning loop) | High |
| Wafer Fabrication (Foundry / IDM) | Execute 500–1,500 process steps to transform a blank wafer into a wafer full of functional die, integrating all upstream tiers into a manufacturable, yielding process | FEOL (front-end-of-line): shallow trench isolation (STI, LOCOS etch + oxide fill); gate dielectric (thermal oxide or High-K ALD); gate electrode (polysilicon or metal gate — HKMG, RMG); source/drain formation (ion implant, spike anneal at 1050°C, millisecond laser anneal); PFET/NFET epitaxial S/D (SiGe for PFET strain, SiP for NFET strain); contact formation (Co or W silicide, ALD TiN liner, W CVD fill); FinFET / GAAFET formation: Si fin etch (RIE), SiGe/Si nanosheet superlattice growth, selective SiGe etch (nanosheet release); BEOL (back-end-of-line): Cu dual-damascene (dielectric deposition → litho → etch → Ta/TaN barrier → Cu ECD → CMP, repeat ×15–20 metal layers); advanced packaging process integration: CoWoS (die attach on RDL + silicon interposer, HBM join, compression bonding); wafer bonding: SoIC (Cu-Cu hybrid bonding, <1µm pitch via CMP + annealing) | TSMC N3E process (2nm effective gate length, GAAFET transition at N2); Intel 18A process (RibbonFET GAAFET + PowerVia backside power delivery); Samsung SF3 process (MBCFET GAAFET, 3nm); CoWoS-L (local silicon interconnect, 2.5D interposer for HBM3e + GPU); SoIC-X (3D face-to-face bonding, 9µm Cu-Cu pitch); process design kit (PDK, SPICE model + DRC deck + standard cell library per node) | Yes | Very High | Process IP + Scale + Certification (leading-edge foundry: TSMC has ~92% share of <5nm revenue; switching foundry requires 18–24 month PDK re-design; node leadership = first-mover compounding) | High (cyclical) |
| Advanced Packaging (CoWoS / SoIC) | Integrate multiple die onto a silicon interposer (2.5D) or via 3D hybrid bonding and join HBM stacks beside the GPU — the fastest-changing and most capacity-constrained step in the AI hardware chain | Advanced 2.5D (CoWoS): Si interposer manufacturing (RDL litho + Cu via TSV, 10µm pitch) → die bonding (thermo-compression bonding, TCB) → HBM3e stack join → resin mold → substrate attach; 3D-IC (SoIC): wafer-level Cu-Cu hybrid bonding (CMP planarization → bonding at 200°C → annealing) → 9µm pitch interconnect; FOWLP (fan-out wafer-level package): die placement → molded reconstituted wafer → RDL litho → solder bump; chiplet integration: die-to-die interface (UCIe 1.0 / HBI) → multi-die reticle stitching | CoWoS-S (silicon interposer, 4×HBM3e + GPU die, TSMC CoWoS); CoWoS-L (local Si interconnect variant, for wider interposer); SoIC-X (9µm Cu-Cu pitch face-to-face bonding, TSMC 3D-IC); FOWLP/InFO module (fan-out RDL, TSMC InFO_PoP); HBM3e stack join (12-hi, 36GB — Samsung/SK Hynix/Micron supply die); UCIe chiplet interconnect (<10µm bump pitch, 1Tbps/mm) | Yes | High | Process IP + Scale (CoWoS capacity at TSMC is the primary GPU-shipment bottleneck; SoIC hybrid bonding is a deep process-IP moat) | High |
| OSAT — Assembly & Test | Dice wafers, wire-bond or flip-chip die into conventional packages, and assemble the system-in-package at volume — the high-scale outsourced assembly and test tier beneath advanced packaging | Die preparation: laser dicing (stealth dicing, minimal kerf) → UV tape stretch → die attach film (DAF) laminate; legacy packaging: wire bonding (Au or Cu, 25µm wire, <50µm pitch), overmold (epoxy novolac), solder ball attach (SAC305 BGA); flip-chip: UBM (under-bump metallization) → solder bump reflow (SnAg, 150µm pitch) → underfill dispense + cure → substrate mount; final test handling and tape-and-reel | Wire-bond BGA / QFN package; flip-chip BGA module; system-in-package (SiP); module assembly + final test | Partial | High | Scale + Customer lock-in (OSAT margins are thin but volume and qualified capacity create switching costs; ASE/Amkor/JCET dominate) | Medium |
| Test & Burn-In | Electrically verify every die and packaged device; stress-screen for latent defects; characterize performance bins for yield optimization and product segmentation | Wafer-level test: parametric probe (Kelvin, CV, IV curves) → functional die map; WLBI (wafer-level burn-in): +30–40% above Vdd, 125°C, 24–48 hours → screen early-life failures (infant mortality from crystallographic defects in SiC); ATE functional test: vector-based test (ATPG patterns, 30–100 billion test cycles per chip), parallel test (64–128 DUT in parallel); scan chain test: JTAG / IEEE 1149.1 boundary scan → structural test of logic chains; memory test: BIST (built-in self-test), row/column repair (laser fusing of redundant rows), data retention test; SLT (system-level test): plug chip into socket + run real OS + workload → catch multi-chip interaction bugs (critical for AI GPUs at scale); thermal screening: burn-in chamber (150°C, 96h for automotive AEC-Q100 Grade 0); binning: characterize max frequency and power at each voltage corner → grade into SKU bins (H100 → H100 NVL → A100 class) | Wafer prober (TEL P-12, Cascade Microtech CM300xi); WLBI system (Aehr WaferPak, accommodates 300mm SiC wafer, 100A+ per site); ATE (Advantest V93000 Wave Scale Mx, Teradyne UltraFLEX+, 2,048 digital channels per tester); burn-in board (device-specific; reflow-soldered test socket + voltage regulator, 256 DUT capacity); SLT cluster (custom rack with production GPU board, 16 DUT per rack); IC test socket (Yamaichi, Wells Electronics — precision pogo-pin or compression mount); laser repair station (fuse-blowing for DRAM row repair) | Partial | Low–Medium | Certification (automotive AEC-Q100 / AEC-Q101 qualification for burn-in condition requires customer and IATF audits; WLBI for SiC has no alternative process — Aehr near-monopoly on this specific market) | Low–Medium |

## Structural Gaps

### Gap: EUV Photoresist — 2–3 Supplier Global Concentration
EUV chemically-amplified photoresist (CAR) and metal-oxide resist (MOR) for High-NA EUV is produced by JSR (Japan, taken private by government-directed acquisition in 2023), Shin-Etsu, and Tokyo Ohka Kogyo (TOK). The US has no significant EUV resist production. Each resist formulation is co-developed with TSMC/ASML per node — it is a node-specific product requiring 2–3 years of co-optimization. No investable US pure-play exists. **Watch for:** Any Shin-Etsu resist revenue breakdown; TOK (5301.T) ADR or reporting change; US CHIPS Act resist localization program.

### Gap: Advanced Test — Advantest Dominance
The GPU and HBM test market at leading-edge nodes is effectively controlled by Advantest (Japan-listed, 6857.T) via the V93000 Wave Scale Mx system. Teradyne has the J750 and UltraFLEX+ but loses share at leading-edge AI chip test. The largest gap: no US-listed company is the primary tester for H200/B200 class GPUs. **Watch for:** Advantest quarterly guidance; Teradyne Semiconductor Test division GPU commentary.

### Gap: GaN Power Device Pure-Play
Wolfspeed is SiC-focused. MACOM (MTSI) has GaN-on-SiC for RF/microwave but not power conversion at scale. Qorvo (QRVO) has GaN PA for RF. No pure-play GaN power device company is US-listed at scale. The 800VDC data center rectifier and EV fast-charging markets are the two largest GaN power TAMs. **Watch for:** STMicro or Infineon GaN power design win in data center rectifier; any GaN power SPAC/IPO.

### Gap: CoWoS / Advanced Packaging Capacity
TSMC controls the CoWoS (chip-on-wafer-on-substrate) interposer process for AI GPU packaging. There is no independent OSAT or foundry that can produce CoWoS at leading-edge specs — it requires the same toolset and PDK alignment as wafer fab. TSMC's CoWoS capacity is the primary bottleneck for GPU shipments when demand exceeds supply. **Watch for:** TSMC CoWoS capacity expansion announcements; AMKR or ASX advanced packaging capex for alternative interposer solutions.

## Key Questions to Answer Before Writing the Sector Framework

1. Does TSMC's CoWoS monopoly on advanced packaging represent a permanent structural chokepoint for AI compute scaling — or can Amkor, ASE, and Samsung OSATs develop a credible alternative 2.5D process within 3 years?
2. Is the High-NA EUV transition (0.55 NA, ASML EXE:5000) a 2026–2028 event that TSMC and Samsung execute in parallel, or does it become a new node of differentiation that widens the foundry gap further?
3. With SiC substrate supply from Wolfspeed constrained and China (Tianyu Semiconductor, SICC) rapidly expanding SiC capacity, is there a 2026–2028 window where non-Chinese SiC MOSFET makers lose pricing power to Chinese vertical integration?
4. Does the custom ASIC market (Google TPU, Amazon Trainium, Meta MTIA, Microsoft Maia) represent a structural shift away from merchant GPU, or is it a complementary layer that leaves NVDA's volume intact while removing margin upside?
5. Is Arm's server CPU market share trajectory (Graviton4, Neoverse V3, Ampere Altra) durable enough to create a 30–40% x86 displacement scenario by 2030 — and what does that mean for Intel's IDM model?
6. When does AI-driven demand for HBM create the next substrate-level bottleneck after the current CoWoS ramp — is it HBM die supply, ABF substrate supply, or TSMC packaging capacity?
