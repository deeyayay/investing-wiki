## Investment Thesis

> **Thesis established:** May 31, 2026
> **Last validated:** May 31, 2026
> **Drift status:** On track — initial thesis established; monitor for first AI data center revenue validation in 2026

Navitas Semiconductor designs GaN (gallium nitride) and SiC (silicon carbide) power ICs under a fabless model, outsourcing wafer production while owning the architecture of what they call GaNFast — a monolithic integration of GaN power transistor, gate driver, control logic, sensing, and protection on a single die. The competitive edge over traditional multi-chip GaN modules (used by Infineon, TI) is system density and switching speed: GaNFast can operate at 10–100× the switching frequency of silicon IGBTs, enabling smaller magnetics, lower board area, and higher efficiency across the conversion chain. The company is mid-execution on "Navitas 2.0," a pivot away from mobile/consumer fast-charging (which drove early revenue but carries thin margins and short design cycles) toward AI data center power, industrial electrification, and EV on-board chargers.

The bull case is a structural alignment: NVIDIA's 800VDC AI factory architecture requires a complete redesign of rack power distribution, and Navitas is the only publicly named GaN partner in that program. Legacy 54V server power distribution cannot efficiently serve 1kW+ GPU thermal envelopes — 800VDC eliminates the 12V-to-54V intermediate conversion stage, cuts I²R losses, and requires SiC MOSFETs and high-voltage GaN devices that Navitas designs specifically. If NVIDIA's Kyber / Rubin Ultra racks go to volume production in 2026–2027, Navitas has revenue visibility that most of its pure-play power semi peers lack. The customer pipeline has already nearly doubled to $2.4B (end of 2024 vs. $1.25B end of 2023), suggesting design-win momentum is real.

The moat is narrow but defensible in the near term: Navitas holds 200+ GaN patents and the GaNFast monolithic architecture is not easily replicated — competitors offering GaN-on-SiC (a competing substrate choice) or multi-chip modules face different integration trade-offs. The GlobalFoundries partnership (Burlington, VT; expected to begin production late 2026) adds US-based manufacturing optionality relevant to federal AI infrastructure spend and supply-chain security requirements. However, the moat's durability is unproven — Infineon (IFX) is the most formidable competitor, is already named as an NVIDIA 800VDC partner, is profitable, and has a 10× revenue base.

Key risks are structural: Navitas is pre-profitability on a GAAP basis, with the mobile/consumer revenue leg declining faster than data center ramps. The CEO transition (Gene Sheridan → Chris Allexandre, Sept 2025) and CFO departure (Todd Glickman, 2026) represent back-to-back leadership changes during a pivotal window. Allexandre brings credible large-company experience (ran Renesas' $2.5B power management unit) but has no track record at NVTS specifically. Positive EBITDA is targeted for 2026, but material P&L from AI data center is guided for 2027 — the trough is long.

**Key moat:** Monolithic GaN-on-Si integration (GaNFast architecture); 200+ GaN patents; named NVIDIA 800VDC partner; GlobalFoundries US GaN foundry agreement (late 2026).

**Key risks:** Pre-profitability; CEO + CFO transitions in 2025–2026; mobile revenue decline outpacing data center ramp timing; Infineon (IFX) is larger, profitable, and also a named NVIDIA partner; GaN-on-Si vs. GaN-on-SiC substrate debate remains unresolved at very high power levels.
