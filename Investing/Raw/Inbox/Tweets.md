After Memory, I Am Watching Connectivity
JP Insights (@Aktiehedonist) — Jun 5, 2026
Source: https://x.com/i/status/2062867161660420259
[Parts 1 & 2 of 2]

Key takeaways
The AI bottleneck keeps moving. After compute and memory, connectivity — meaning everything that moves data between the chips — is the next layer that could reprice from a component into a constraint.
A real bottleneck shows up in the economics, in expanding margins, multi-year customer commitments and supply that cannot keep up. This earnings season those signals are arriving across the chain, though unevenly.
Margins are the dividing line. When revenue grows but margins stay flat, the market is paying for a capacity ramp, and pricing power has not been proven.
Who owns the architecture as the standards keep shifting is still unsettled, so the edge is in filtering for the companies that can follow the constraint as it moves, and staying patient on price after the recent run.

The market has already found memory. Micron, SK Hynix and the rest of the HBM chain did not re-rate because investors rediscovered semiconductor cyclicality. They re-rated because memory stopped behaving like an ordinary component and started behaving like a hard constraint, something the cycle has to pay up for rather than simply ride through.

The question I keep turning over is whether connectivity is starting through the same door. Matt Murphy made that case out loud at his Computex keynote. His point was simple. The bottleneck in AI is moving from the chips to the data that has to travel between them, and the second problem is now the harder one to solve. The shape differs from one company to the next, and the proof is further along in some parts of the chain than others. But the pattern is getting harder to wave away.

A larger cluster needs more than GPUs. It needs those GPUs to talk to each other, and that communication layer is becoming one of the hardest parts of the system. Data has to move inside the rack, between racks, across a data center, and eventually between regions. Every one of those steps has to be built: cables, switches, retimers, optical engines, transceivers, lasers, DSPs, pluggables, transport systems, and the software to hold it all together. The AI factory is compute that has been wired together, and the wiring is starting to look like the part that binds the whole thing.

The evidence is no longer coming from one corner of the market. It is coming from several layers at once. $AVGO and $MRVL are talking about the architecture. $ANET and $CSCO are talking about the network. $ALAB and $CRDO are showing the rack-level and interconnect side. $LITE, $COHR, $GLW and $APH are showing the physical optical layer. $FN is showing the constraint on the factory floor. $AXTI and Aixtron are showing that the pressure may be reaching further upstream still.

Why memory is the right comparison, and where it breaks:
Memory was concentrated, supply and demand mechanics were legible, and HBM ran in a fairly straight line to margins and earnings. Connectivity is messier — it spans switch silicon, networking systems, active electrical cables, retimers, DSPs, transceivers, lasers, silicon photonics, optical transport, manufacturing capacity, substrates and tools. The value will not land evenly. Some names only brush the theme. Others sit on scarce architecture, qualified supply or a customer roadmap that cannot be swapped out.

The mechanism: every time a cluster gets bigger, the communication burden grows faster than compute does. More GPUs means more traffic between GPUs, more racks means more traffic between racks, more inference means more data in constant motion. The compute buildout creates a networking problem → interconnect problem → optics problem, which pulls lasers, pluggables, optical engines, DSPs, switches, retimers, test, qualification and process know-how into the demand picture.

Evidence one: the architecture you cannot swap out

$AVGO (Broadcom): The platform. AI semiconductor revenue reached $10.8B in Q2 fiscal, +143% YoY, with custom accelerators and AI networking as drivers, inside total revenue of $22.2B. Risk: valuation — stock fell sharply on that record quarter because management did not raise full-year AI target.

$ANET (Arista): Clearest live example. Q1 revenue $2.71B, +35%. Raised full-year AI revenue target to $3.5B (>2x prior year). Purchase commitments jumped to $8.9B from $6.8B in a single quarter — committing ahead of demand because it believes the shortage is real and lasting.

$CSCO (Cisco): Q3 FY2026 revenue record at $15.8B, +12%. AI infrastructure orders from hyperscalers: $5.3B YTD, full-year target raised to ~$9B (~4.5x prior year). Silicon One switch silicon and Acacia coherent optics both being pulled into the buildout. Caution: $9B AI orders inside a $60B+ revenue company — constraint moves Cisco far less than a pure play.

$ALAB (Astera Labs): Rack-level layer. Q1 revenue $308.4M, +93% YoY, gross margin >76%. New Scorpio fabric switch starting to ship; PCIe 6 already >1/3 of revenue. Risk: valuation assumes future arrives on time — CXL timing and Scorpio ramp are where that assumption gets tested.

$CRDO (Credo): Purest read on data-movement. FY2026 revenue >3x to $1.3B; Q4 alone $437M (>full prior year), gross margin ~68%. CEO: "connectivity has become the limiting factor in AI infrastructure as clusters scale into hundreds of thousands of GPUs." Open question: does Credo follow the constraint as copper's reach shrinks? Optical roadmap matters more than any single quarter. Stock fell on strong print — expectations reset.

$AMD (not a basket holding): Pushing open camp through UALink, Ultra Ethernet, Pensando networking — trying to keep the scale-up fabric from becoming one vendor's private property.

Evidence two: it is reaching the income statement

$LITE (Lumentum): Q3 revenue +90% YoY to record $808M, with operating margin up ~700 bps in a single quarter. Margin climbing alongside revenue — that is what separates capturing the constraint from merely shipping into it.

$GLW (Corning): Optical comms business +36% YoY in Q1, eighth straight quarter of growth. Two more hyperscalers signed multi-year agreements on the scale of the ~$6B Meta deal; Nvidia partnership landed. Management lifted long-range plan toward $40B annual run rate by 2030. Customers reserving physical glass years ahead — the behavior a real constraint produces. Risk: handful of very large buyers carrying the growth.

$APH (Amphenol): Q1 sales +58% to $7.62B; adjusted operating margin 27.3%. Orders record at $9.4B, book-to-bill 1.24. IT datacom ~41% of revenue, growing ~80% organically on AI interconnect demand. Risk: concentration and heavier balance sheet after CommScope deal.

$COHR (Coherent): Revenue $1.81B, +21%. Datacenter and communications now ~75% of company; order book stretched into 2028; margins expanded. Open question: gets durable and profitable enough to lift the whole company, or stays a strong pocket inside a complicated one.

$FN (Fabrinet): Revenue record $1.214B, +39%. Datacom shipments held back by component shortages even as demand ran ahead of capacity. When a contract manufacturer is held back by supply rather than demand, that is physical capacity talking.

$AAOI (Applied Optoelectronics): Q1 revenue +51% to $151M; datacenter revenue +154% on first volume 800G hyperscaler shipments. Gross margin stuck at ~29% while company still lost money. Being inside the bottleneck and capturing its economics are different things. Management targets mid-30s gross margin by year-end, past 40% in 2027 as 800G/1.6T mix builds — that promise is the test.

Evidence three: the constraint reaches past the rack

$CIEN (Ciena): Q2 revenue +40% to $1.57B; full-year guidance raised to $6.3B; backlog record $7.7B. Stock fell 17% on the day — raise was not large enough for a price that had assumed perfection. Two cloud customers already ~1/3 of revenue.

$NOK (Nokia): AI and cloud customers +49%, €1B of new orders booked. Optical +20%. But AI/cloud is still only ~8% of the group and company-wide operating margin sat near 6%. Has to show up in margin before the re-rating is fully earned.

$MRVL (Marvell): Hybrid spanning custom silicon, DSPs, interconnect and optical. Q1 revenue $2.42B, +28%; data center 76% of total. Raised FY2027 and FY2028 outlook on "exceptional AI bookings"; Murphy expects growth to accelerate each quarter. Trap: generalist rarely captures as much as a specialist; gap between $0.04 GAAP and $0.80 non-GAAP shows how much still runs through acquisitions and integration.

Evidence four: it may run deeper than the obvious layer

$AXTI (AXT): InP substrate for high-speed lasers. Q1 2026 revenue $26.9M, +39%; InP crossing half of sales for first time; InP backlog record $100M; non-GAAP gross margin 29.9% vs 21.5% prior quarter. Still loss-making but narrowing. $632.5M equity raise to fund Tongmei InP capacity (~double in 2026, another double in 2027). Key risk: shipments depend on China export permits — backlog is only as real as the licenses that let it leave the building.

Aixtron (AIX.DE): Equipment used to grow compound semiconductor material. Revenue fell 47% and posted operating loss (cyclical lumpiness of selling big machines in batches). But order intake +~30% to ~€171M, >65% from optoelectronics; full-year guidance raised toward €560M. Lumentum placed a multi-system order for AI optics tooling during the quarter. Chain it points at laid end to end: GPU → switch → cable → optical module → laser → wafer → epitaxy tool.

Conclusion (Part 1):
Connectivity can get the memory treatment, but not evenly — and the unevenness is the point. Memory was cleaner (concentrated supplier base, visible pricing power, legible operating leverage). Connectivity is wider and noisier. The easy mistake is to buy anything that says optical, AI networking or interconnect. The better discipline: does the company have pricing power? Does it control architecture? Does it hold qualified capacity a customer cannot easily replace? Is demand showing up in backlog, deferred revenue, purchase commitments, gross margins and cash flow — or only in management commentary?

What would confirm it (Part 2):

1. Supply constraints at several chain points simultaneously — most clearly ticked. $FN shipping less than it could due to component shortages; $CIEN carrying record $7.7B backlog; $ANET committed $8.9B to chips/materials ahead of demand. Different companies, different seats, same message: supply cannot keep up.

2. Margin expansion (most important) — field splitting usefully. $LITE added ~700 bps operating margin in a single quarter. $COHR and $APH expanded margins while growing fast. $AVGO, $ALAB and $CRDO compounding at high, steady gross margins. $AAOI sits on the other side: growing 51% with margin stuck near 29%, still losing money. That split marks off the names capturing the constraint from those merely exposed to it.

3. Customer behavior — prepayments, long contracts, reservations. Already arriving: $GLW signed multiple multi-year hyperscaler agreements; $COHR's order book reaches into 2028; $CSCO booked $5.3B of AI orders against a $9B full-year target. Customers do not lock up supply that far ahead on a hunch.

4. Architecture lock-in — strongest names becoming harder to replace. Still genuinely open. $AVGO embedded in custom roadmaps; $ALAB watching Scorpio grow into its largest product line. But standards still being fought over (copper vs optical, open camp vs Nvidia), so who owns the layer in three years is not settled.

5. Theme surviving a bad stock reaction — the last few weeks were the test. $CRDO, $AVGO and $CIEN all reported strong quarters and all sold off ($CIEN -17% on a beat and raise). The businesses kept delivering while the multiples reset. The thesis breaks when the numbers break. A falling multiple is a different problem.

Where this leaves me:
Connectivity is not the new memory in any clean one-to-one way — memory had the easier setup (visible bottleneck, modelable pricing power), while connectivity is more fragmented, more technical and more uneven. But the setup is starting to rhyme. The larger the AI factory gets, the more its internal nervous system decides how well it runs, and that nervous system is connectivity. The winners: companies that control architecture, scarce supply, high-performance interconnect, real optical components, networking systems or qualified manufacturing capacity.
