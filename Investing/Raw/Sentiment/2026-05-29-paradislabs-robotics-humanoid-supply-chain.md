# Robotics: The Next AI Frontier
**Author:** Paradis Labs (@ParadisLabs) · May 29, 2026

AI's next frontier will be Robotics and Humanoids.
The past decade has seen rapid AI adoption in the structured digital world.
Those LLM breakthroughs now enable more general-purpose learning and more reliable operation in messy, unstructured human environments.
That same paradigm shift that gave us conversational AI is now being wired into machines that can see, decide, and move.
Humanoids.

$NVDA CEO Jensen Huang (May 20, 2026): "The world is rebuilding computing for agentic AI and robotic physical AI"

Traditionally, humanoid costs have been the bottleneck to mass adoption.
But aggressive supply chain scaling has meant humanoid avg. unit prices are expected to fall from ~$100,000 today to ~$25,000 by 2030 (Goldman Sachs).
Coupled with global demographic shifts acting as a significant tailwind to increased humanoid sales volumes in the coming years:
Where sectors like agriculture, healthcare, construction and manufacturing face severe labour market tightness & wage inflation. Driven by people no longer wanting to work in those fields anymore compared to just 10 years ago.

This further opens the door for humanoid robots to scale:
Many of the "manual labour" jobs require the human skillset, by nature - from dextrous fingers for healthcare and agriculture, to speed and precision in logistics and construction.
Humanoids can and will fill the labour gap - 24/7 round the clock work with no mistakes and no days off to go to the dentist. All at one flat fee for the cost of ~1-2 worker's annual salaries (currently), plus the odd repair cost here and there.

That's the blue-sky scenario.

VC firms and governments know this, and are all piling in at record rates to secure a slice of the pie. Evidenced by an exponential rise in robotics and humanoid funding over the past ~5 years:
- VC humanoid funding 3x'd from 2023–2025 to $40.7B
- China has a $138B fund for AI and robotics
- Startups like Figure AI (U.S.) are raising billion dollar funding rounds, resulting in multi-billion dollar valuations.

This structural inflection point in the (1) technology, and (2) funding landscape, is evidenced by humanoid TAM estimates from leading institutions:
- Morgan Stanley: $5 Trillion TAM by 2050 (Across the value chain)
- Bank of America: 1M annual humanoid robot shipments by 2035 vs. ~90k in 2026
- BCG: ~1M annual units by 2030

All of this raises the question: How do I make money from this?

In my opinion, the investable money today is in the supply chain, not the headline robot-makers:
Most marquee OEMs are private or buried deep within conglomerates.
Figure ~$39B, Physical Intelligence ~$5.6B, Apptronik ~$5.5B, Skild AI ~$14B.
And other makers/integrators such as $TSLA, $AMZN, $AAPL and BYD have legacy revenue lines, resulting in robotics becoming a relatively miniscule line-item relative to their scale and scope.

So my view is that the market entry points are in the component suppliers that build up the humanoids. E.g. rotary/linear actuators, sensing/perception systems, semiconductors, and memory.

As of writing this, companies specializing in rotary/linear actuators specifically seems to be the best way to get pure-play exposure to the humanoid supply chain:
- Barclays, Bank of America, and McKinsey independently estimate that actuators account for roughly 40–60% of a humanoid's BOM.

However it's widely known that China currently has the lions-share of capacity for rotary & linear actuators:

So for investors who seek non-Chinese exposure, this would be a non-exhaustive list of suppliers that sit in the actuator bucket:

### Rotary actuators — precision reducers
- **Harmonic Drive Systems** (Japan · 6324) — harmonic reducers used in rotary joints like wrists and arms
- **Nabtesco** (Japan · 6268) — RV/cycloidal precision reducers for high-torque rotary joints such as hips and shoulders
- **Timken** (US · $TKR) — cycloidal/worm gearing + Rollon linear motion for actuators

### Linear actuators — roller / ball screws
- **THK** (Japan · 6481) — ball screws + linear-guides
- **NSK** (Japan · 6471) — ball screws + bearings
- **SKF** (Sweden · SKF-B) — roller screws + precision bearings
- **Schaeffler** (Germany · SFFLY) — planetary roller screws for linear joints + developing rotary actuators and bearings
- **AMETEK** (US · $AME) — screws + compact linear actuators
- **Hiwin** (Taiwan · 2049) — ball screws, linear guides + harmonic drives

### Actuator motors — frameless torque / servo
- **Regal Rexnord** (US · $RRX) — frameless torque motors, ball screws / linear actuators
- **Moog** (US · MOG.A) — frameless motors + electromechanical rotary and linear actuators
- **Parker Hannifin** (US · $PH) — frameless servo motors + electromechanical rotary/linear actuators
- **Allient / Allied Motion** (US · $ALNT) — frameless and servo motors, drives and integrated motion modules
- **Novanta** (US · $NOVT) — frameless direct-drive motors and precision encoders
- **Nidec** (Japan · 6594) — BLDC/servo motors plus reducers for integrated joint modules

There's also a range of motion/servo houses where humanoid exposure is relatively minimal. E.g. Mitsubishi Electric (6503), Sumitomo Heavy (6302), ABB, Siemens, and Fanuc (6954). But those names (like the integrators e.g. Tesla), have widely diversified operations where robotics is a small piece of the puzzle.

Many will view now as being too early given the current barriers to commercial humanoid scale:
Unproven ROI and expensive repairs; relatively immature actuator supply chains (BofA), sensors, and reducers; absent safety standards; robots too fragile for 24/7 uptime; and limited battery life.

None of these are solved by better demo videos, but are being actively worked on by almost every name in the supply chain.

On the earliness of investing now:
I tend to agree purely from a capital allocation perspective given that we're in the eye of the memory storm ($SNDK, $MU) and the wider AI supercycle with segments like photonics ($SIVE, $LITE), advanced packaging ($AEHR), CPUs ($INTC, $AMD), and neoclouds ($NBIS).

However, I believe that now is the right time to be eyeing up potential opportunities for the forecasted ~H1'28 volume ramp.
